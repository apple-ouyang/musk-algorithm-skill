<span id="ref1121134"></span>[PayPal](/money/PayPal) and formed

[Tesla](/money/Tesla-Motors). In addition, Musk acquired [Twitter (later

## Early life

Musk was born to a South African father and a Canadian mother. He
displayed an early talent for [computers](/technology/computer) and
entrepreneurship. At age 12 he created a [video
game](/topic/electronic-game) and sold it to a computer magazine. In
1988, after obtaining a Canadian passport, Musk left South Africa
because he was unwilling to support [apartheid](/topic/apartheid)
through [compulsory military service](/topic/conscription) and because
he sought the greater economic opportunities available in the [United
States](/place/United-States).

<span id="ref341081" level="1"></span>

## PayPal and SpaceX

Musk attended [Queen’s University](/topic/Queens-University-at-Kingston)
in Kingston, Ontario, and in 1992 he transferred to the [University of
Pennsylvania](/topic/University-of-Pennsylvania), Philadelphia, where he
received bachelor’s degrees in [physics](/science/physics-science) and
[economics](/money/economics) in 1997. He enrolled in graduate school in
physics at [Stanford University](/topic/Stanford-University) in
California, but he left after only two days because he felt that the
[Internet](/technology/Internet) had much more potential to change
society than work in physics. In 1995, he founded
<span id="ref1121137"></span>[Zip2](/money/Zip2), a company that
provided maps and business directories to online newspapers. In 1999,
Zip2 was bought by the computer manufacturer [Compaq](/money/Compaq) for
\$307 million, and Musk then founded an online financial services
company, X.com, which later became PayPal, which specialized in
transferring money online. The online auction [eBay](/money/eBay) bought
PayPal in 2002 for \$1.5 billion.

Musk was long convinced that for life to survive, humanity has to become
a multiplanet species, but he was dissatisfied with the great expense of
[rocket](/technology/rocket-jet-propulsion-device-and-vehicle)
launchers. In 2002, he founded Space Exploration Technologies (SpaceX)
to make more affordable rockets. Its first two rockets were the
<span id="ref1121138"></span>[Falcon](/technology/Falcon-launch-vehicle)
1 (first launched in 2006) and the larger Falcon 9 (first launched in
2010), which were designed to cost much less than competing rockets. A
third rocket, the <span id="ref1187434"></span>[Falcon
Heavy](/topic/Falcon-Heavy) (first launched in 2018), was designed to
carry 117,000 pounds (53,000 kg) to orbit, nearly twice as much as its
largest competitor, the [Boeing Company](/money/Boeing-Company)’s Delta
IV Heavy, for one-third the cost. The Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy
eventually dominated the [launch vehicle](/technology/launch-vehicle)
market, and in 2024 more than half of the world’s orbital launches were
done by SpaceX.

SpaceX has been developing and testing the successor to the Falcon 9 and
the Falcon Heavy: the Super Heavy–Starship system. The Super Heavy first
stage would be capable of lifting 100,000 kg (220,000 pounds) to low
Earth orbit. The payload would be the Starship, a spacecraft designed
for providing fast
[transportation](/technology/transportation-technology) between cities
on Earth and building bases on the Moon and Mars.

SpaceX also developed the
<span id="ref1121140"></span>[Dragon](/topic/Dragon-spacecraft)
spacecraft, which carries supplies to the [International Space
Station](/topic/International-Space-Station) (ISS). Dragon can carry as
many as seven astronauts, and it had a crewed flight carrying astronauts
Doug Hurley and Robert Behnken to the ISS in 2020. The first test
flights of the Super Heavy–Starship system launched in 2020. In addition
to being CEO of SpaceX, Musk was also chief designer in building the
Falcon rockets, Dragon, and Starship. SpaceX is contracted to build the
lander for the astronauts returning to the Moon by 2026 as part of
[NASA](/topic/NASA)’s [Artemis](/topic/Artemis-program) space program.

SpaceX has also developed the
[Starlink](/topic/Starlink) satellite network, which
provides [broadband](/technology/broadband-technology) internet services
globally. Musk launched the project in 2015 in part to bankroll his
ambition of establishing a human colony on Mars. In March 2018,
the [Federal Communications
Commission](/topic/Federal-Communications-Commission) (FCC) gave SpaceX
the go-ahead to launch 4,425 satellites into [low Earth
orbit](/technology/low-Earth-orbit). A Falcon 9 rocket carried the first
batch of [satellites](/science/satellite) into low Earth orbit in May
2019. Beta testing commenced in 2020, and commercial services were
rolled out in 2021. As of 2025 there are about 7,000 working Starlink
satellites, accounting for more than half of all active satellites.
Starlink is a [megaconstellation](/technology/megaconstellation), or
satellite Internet constellation, with more than five million
subscribers as of 2025. In addition to its civilian services, the
company operates Starshield, a military-grade version of Starlink, and
is building a secure satellite system for the U.S. military.

## Tesla

Musk had long been interested in the possibilities of electric cars, and
in 2004 he became one of the major funders of
<span id="ref1121136"></span>[Tesla Motors](/money/Tesla-Motors) (later
renamed Tesla), an electric car company founded by entrepreneurs [Martin
Eberhard and Marc
Tarpenning](/money/Martin-Eberhard-and-Marc-Tarpenning). In 2006, Tesla
introduced its first car, the
<span id="ref1121139"></span>[Roadster](/technology/Tesla-Roadster),
which could travel 245 miles (394 km) on a single charge. Unlike most
previous electric vehicles, which Musk thought were stodgy and
uninteresting, it was a sports car that could go from 0 to 60 mph (97
km/h) in less than four seconds. In 2010, the company’s [initial public
offering](/money/ipo-initial-public-offering) raised about \$226
million. Two years later Tesla introduced the Model S sedan, which was
acclaimed by automotive critics for its performance and design. The
company won further praise for its Model X luxury SUV, which went on the
market in 2015. The Model 3, a less-expensive vehicle, went into
production in 2017 and became the best-selling electric car of all time.

## Historic compensation plan

In November 2025, Musk secured shareholder approval for a new \$1
trillion compensation package from Tesla, one of the largest in
corporate history. Under the plan Musk could receive stock awards worth
up to that amount over the next decade if he meets a series of ambitious
financial and operational targets. They include increasing Tesla’s
market capitalization to about \$8.5 trillion—roughly six times its 2025
value—and expanding its robotics and autonomous-driving divisions. The
vote, which followed months of debate over [corporate
governance](/money/corporate-governance) and executive pay, reaffirmed
Musk’s influence at Tesla and his status as the world’s richest person.

<span id="ref350362" level="1"></span>

## X (formerly <span id="ref1302883"></span>[Twitter](/money/Twitter))

Musk joined the [social media](/topic/social-media) service
[Twitter](/money/Twitter) in 2009, and, as @elonmusk, he became one of
the most popular accounts on the site, with more than 85 million
followers as of 2022. He expressed reservations about Tesla’s being
publicly traded, and in August 2018 he made a series of tweets about
taking the company private at a value of \$420 a share, noting that he
had “secured funding.” (The value of \$420 was seen as a joking
reference to April 20, a day celebrated by devotees of
[cannabis](/plant/cannabis-plant).) The following month the U.S.
<span id="ref1308505"></span>[Securities and Exchange
Commission](/money/Securities-and-Exchange-Commission) (SEC) sued Musk
for securities fraud, alleging that the tweets were “false and
misleading.” Shortly thereafter Tesla’s board rejected the SEC’s
proposed settlement, reportedly because Musk had threatened to resign.
The news sent Tesla stock plummeting, and a harsher deal was ultimately
accepted. Its terms included Musk’s stepping down as
[chairman](/money/chairperson) for three years, though he was allowed to
continue as CEO; his tweets were to be preapproved by Tesla lawyers, and
fines of \$20 million for both Tesla and Musk were levied.

Musk was critical of Twitter’s commitment to principles of [free
speech](/topic/freedom-of-speech), in light of the company’s
content-moderation policies. Early in April 2022, Twitter’s filings with
the SEC disclosed that Musk had bought more than 9% of the company.
Shortly thereafter Twitter announced that Musk would join the company’s
board, but Musk decided against that and made a bid for the entire
company, at a value of \$54.20 a share, for \$44 billion. Twitter’s
board accepted the deal, which would make him sole owner of the company.
Musk said his plans for the company included “enhancing the product with
new features, making the algorithms open source to increase trust,
defeating the spam bots, and authenticating all humans.” In July 2022,
Musk announced that he was withdrawing his bid, saying that Twitter had
not provided sufficient information about bot accounts and claiming that
the company was in “material breach of multiple provisions” of the
purchase agreement. Bret Taylor, the chair of Twitter’s board of
directors, responded by saying that the company was “committed to
closing the transaction on the price and terms agreed upon with Mr.
Musk.” Twitter sued Musk to force him to buy the company. In September
2022, Twitter’s shareholders voted to accept Musk’s offer. Facing a
legal battle, Musk ultimately proceeded with the deal, and it was
completed in October.

Among Musk’s first acts as Twitter’s owner were to lay off about half
the company and to allow users to purchase for \$8 a month the blue
check-mark verification, which had previously been bestowed by Twitter
upon notable figures. In addition, he disbanded Twitter’s
content-moderation body and reinstated many banned accounts, most
notably that of then-former U.S. president [Donald
Trump](/biography/Donald-Trump), which had been suspended after the
[U.S. Capitol attack on January 6,
2021](/event/January-6-U-S-Capitol-attack). Advertising revenue fell
sharply as many companies withdrew their ads from the service. Musk
changed the name of the company to X in July 2023. (*Tweets* became
*posts* with the change.)

## OpenAI and artificial intelligence

Musk has often expressed fears that unrestricted advancement in
[artificial intelligence](/technology/artificial-intelligence) (AI)
could pose the “biggest existential threat” to humanity. Research lab
[OpenAI](/money/OpenAI), founded as a
[nonprofit](/topic/nonprofit-organization) by Musk, [Sam
Altman](/money/Sam-Altman), and other investors in 2015, was presented
as a more socially responsible project committed to [open
source](/topic/open-source) software and safety. It was also intended to
be a counterbalance to [Google](/money/Google-Inc)’s early lead in AI
development. But Musk exited OpenAI three years later citing a conflict
of interest with software being developed at Tesla. OpenAI’s management
said later that Musk had sought to head a new for-profit arm of the
company or merge it with Tesla. Musk sued OpenAI and major investor
[Microsoft](/money/Microsoft-Corporation) in 2024, accusing them of
straying from the venture’s nonprofit foundations. In February 2025,
Musk and a consortium of investors offered to acquire OpenAI for \$97.4
billion; the company turned down the bid.

Nine months after acquiring Twitter in October 2022, Musk founded a new
AI company called [xAI](/money/xAI), which trains its
[machine-learning](/technology/machine-learning) models on user data
from Twitter (now known as [X](/money/Twitter)) and publicly available
sources. Its primary offering is an AI-powered assistant named Grok, a
rival to OpenAI’s [ChatGPT](/technology/ChatGPT) and Google’s
[Gemini](/technology/Google-Gemini).

## The Boring Company

Frustrated by [Los Angeles](/place/Los-Angeles-California) traffic, Musk
in late 2016 proposed setting up a new infrastructure and tunneling
company to build a low-cost, underground public transport system beneath
the city. Musk’s initial idea was to lower vehicles from the street into
subterranean tunnels, where they would travel on autonomous electric
sleds, called skates, at 125 to 150 mph (200 to 240 km/h). The new
project, called The Boring Company, began as a division of SpaceX and
was [spun off into a separate
business](/money/mergers-and-acquisitions-strategy) in 2018. That same
year the company dug a 1.14-mile (1.8 km) test tunnel near SpaceX’s
headquarters in Hawthorne, [California](/place/California-state). The
company said the tunnel was built for less than \$10 million—a fraction
of the per-mile cost of other tunneling projects in the U.S., which can
run into the billions.

The Boring Company spent about \$47 million to build a pair of tunnels
beneath the Las Vegas Convention Center. Stretching a combined 1.7 miles
(2.7 km), the transportation system, called Loop, opened to the public
in 2021, shuttling passengers across the 200-acre campus in under two
minutes. Musk’s initial vision—cars gliding on autonomous, high-speed
electric skates—was instead replaced by a fleet of human-driven Tesla
cars that ferry passengers through the tunnels at speeds of up to 40 mph
(64 km/h), significantly slower than the originally proposed 125 to 150
mph (200 to 240 km/h). The company has since outlined plans to expand
its network of tunnels in [Las Vegas](/place/Las-Vegas-New-Mexico). It
also proposed tunnel projects in other cities, including
[Baltimore](/place/Baltimore), [Chicago](/place/Chicago), Los Angeles,
and [Washington, D.C.](/place/Washington-DC), which were later discarded
or indefinitely shelved.

The Boring Company also oversees development of the
<span id="ref1187436"></span>[Hyperloop](/technology/Hyperloop), another
[public transportation](/topic/mass-transit) system Musk has championed
for years. In 2013, dissatisfied with the projected \$68 billion cost of
California’s [high-speed rail](/technology/high-speed-rail) project, he
proposed a faster alternative: the Hyperloop, a pneumatic tube in which
a pod carrying 28 passengers would travel the 350 miles (560 km) between
Los Angeles and San Francisco in 35 minutes at a top speed of 760 mph
(1,220 km/h)—nearly the [speed of
sound](/science/speed-of-sound-physics). Musk claimed the Hyperloop
could be built for just \$6 billion and, with pods departing every two
minutes, could serve the six million commuters who travel that route
annually. But he said he couldn’t personally pursue the idea while
running both SpaceX and Tesla.

<span id="ref417832" level="1"></span>

## <span id="ref1343829"></span>Neuralink

In 2016, Musk became fascinated with developing technology that allowed
humans to control computers with their minds. He founded Neuralink with
a team of eight neuroscientists, engineers, and biomedical researchers
to develop neural lace technology (inspired in part by the [science
fiction](/art/science-fiction) of Ian M. Banks), a brain-computer
interface that could be surgically implanted in the human
[brain](/science/brain) and enable wireless interaction with
[computers](/technology/computer) and [mobile
devices](/technology/mobile-telephone) controlled purely by thought.
Building on decades of existing research, the company’s initial goal was
to create a medical device to alleviate neurological conditions such as
[epilepsy](/science/epilepsy) and [paralysis](/science/paralysis).
Musk’s stated long-term goal is to create a brain
[chip](/technology/computer-chip) that would protect individuals from
potentially dangerous applications of artificial intelligence.

Following [animal testing](/science/animal-experimentation), Neuralink
began human [clinical trials](/science/clinical-trial) in 2023, and by
early 2024 had implanted a brain chip called Telepathy in a 29-year-old
with quadriplegia. The recipient could control a
[mouse](/technology/mouse-computer-device) on a screen, browse the web,
and play [chess](/topic/chess) using thought. Later that year, the
company successfully implanted an improved version of the chip in a
second patient, who had sustained a [spinal
injury](/science/spinal-cord-injury). Musk said the company’s next
product, Blindsight, would help restore vision in patients with partial
or complete [blindness](/science/blindness-medicine).

## Politics and DOGE

Musk had described himself as a moderate Democrat, but his views became
much more right-wing after his purchase of X. He publicly endorsed
Republican presidential candidate [Donald
Trump](/biography/Donald-Trump) after [an assassination
attempt](/topic/Assassinations-and-assassination-attempts-involving-U-S-presidents-and-presidential-candidates)
on Trump in July 2024. Through his America [Political Action
Committee](/topic/political-action-committee) (PAC), he became the
country’s largest political donor, giving \$288 million to Trump and
other Republican candidates.

Musk spoke at campaign rallies for Trump and through America PAC set up
a sweepstakes in which he would pay \$1 million a day through [election
day](/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-2024) to one voter in
a key swing state. The action prompted lawsuits alleging that it
amounted to paying people to vote, which is illegal.

Trump promised during his campaign that Musk and entrepreneur [Vivek
Ramaswamy](/biography/Vivek-Ramaswamy) would lead a commission, the
<span id="ref1334279"></span>[Department of Government
Efficiency](/topic/Department-of-Government-Efficiency-United-States)
(DOGE, a reference to the
[cryptocurrency](/money/cryptocurrency-regulation) memecoin Dogecoin),
charged with making the government more efficient. Musk vowed that in
that effort, he would “balance the budget immediately” by cutting \$2
trillion in government spending, which would “involve some temporary
hardship.”

Ramaswamy left DOGE hours after Trump’s inauguration on January 20,
2025. That same day Trump issued an [executive
order](/topic/executive-order) that set up DOGE inside the United States
Digital Service, which was renamed the United States DOGE Service. The
order also stated that each federal agency should have a team of at
least four DOGE employees. Musk became a special government employee, a
designation for someone who works for the government on a temporary
basis. Many of those involved with DOGE are former or current employees
of Musk’s companies.

DOGE immediately went to work. On the afternoon of January 20, a DOGE
team took over the office of the director of the Office of Personnel
Management (OPM), an agency that manages the federal [civil
service](/topic/civil-service), and assumed control of key OPM
databases. More than two million government employees were emailed a
deferred resignation offer. It said that they would not have to return
to their offices if they resigned at the end of September 2025. (The
subject line of the email, “Fork in the Road,” was the same as one used
in an email Musk sent in 2022, offering resignation to Twitter employees
shortly after he took over the service.) DOGE gained access to databases
at the Treasury Department’s Bureau of the Fiscal Service, which
disburses most of the government’s payments.

Musk announced on X that he and Trump would shut down the
<span id="ref1334280"></span>[U.S. Agency for International
Development](/topic/US-Agency-for-International-Development) (USAID),
which administers [foreign aid](/money/foreign-aid). Shortly thereafter
USAID’s website was taken down, most of USAID’s 10,000 employees were
placed on administrative leave, and programs worldwide were suspended.
The [National Institutes of
Health](/topic/National-Institutes-of-Health) (NIH) said it would cut
\$4 billion of “indirect costs,” which support hospitals and
universities of NIH grant recipients. The Institute of Education
Sciences, the research division of the Department of Education, had most
of its contracts canceled.

DOGE’s actions led to a flurry of lawsuits. Critics of DOGE said that it
had precipitated a constitutional crisis, in which the [executive
branch](/topic/executive-government) was violating the [separation of
powers](/topic/separation-of-powers) by not spending the money that
Congress had appropriated for agencies such as USAID and NIH.
Republicans, who controlled Congress, claimed there was nothing unusual
in a new administration reviewing government spending.

Musk stepped down from DOGE on May 28, 2025, a day after he said in an
interview that he was “disappointed” with Trump’s proposed “one big
beautiful bill” for potentially increasing the budget deficit and
undermining the cost-cutting measures DOGE had implemented. The
nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office estimates the spending bill
could increase the federal budget deficit by \$2.4 trillion. In the week
that followed, Musk and Trump traded barbs publicly over the bill. Trump
threatened to revoke Musk’s government contracts, briefly wiping more
than \$150 billion off Tesla’s market capitalization in early June.
