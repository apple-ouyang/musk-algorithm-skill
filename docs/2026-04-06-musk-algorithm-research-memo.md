# Decision Summary

- Most important conclusion: the strongest public source for the ordered five-step method remains the Everyday Astronaut Starbase interview, but the clearest long-form wording and factory anecdotes are reinforced by a readable mirror of Walter Isaacson's *Elon Musk* material.
- Key reasons:
  - Everyday Astronaut gives the clean, ordered five-step list in Musk's engineering context.
  - The Isaacson mirror adds the named-person rule, the 10% add-back deletion rule, Musk's own warning about automating too early, and the fiberglass-strip anecdote.
  - Tesla's official `Master Plan, Part Deux` anchors the wider execution context: first principles, staged scaling, and "designing the machine that makes the machine."
- Need user decision: none.
- Suggested further research: still worth capturing an official or cleaner publisher-backed source for the Isaacson wording and a direct transcript source for the Henry Ford event. Neither is required to improve the skill now.

## What is now strongly supported

### The core five-step sequence

The public corpus now supports this sequence with high confidence:

1. Question or "make the requirements less dumb."
2. Delete parts or process.
3. Simplify and optimize only after deletion.
4. Accelerate cycle time only after the first three steps.
5. Automate last.

### Musk's own strongest usable wording

From the Everyday Astronaut interview summary:

- "Make the requirements less dumb."
- "Try very hard to delete the part or process."
- "Simplify and optimize the design."
- "Accelerate cycle time."
- "Automate."

From the Isaacson mirror wording:

- each requirement should come with the name of the actual person who made it
- smart people's requirements are dangerous because teams question them less
- if you do not add back at least 10% of removed parts/processes, you did not delete enough
- Nevada and Fremont mistakes came from trying to automate every step too early

## Best concrete cases

### Case 1: named-person rule for requirements

The algorithm is not just "question requirements" in the abstract. The stronger version is: refuse department-level vagueness and demand a human owner for each requirement.

Why this matters for the skill:

- it makes the framework operational
- it translates cleanly to product, software, hiring, and operations workflows

### Case 2: fiberglass strip / battery pack anecdote

The mirrored production-hell anecdote is a good example of why step 1 precedes step 5. Musk asks what fiberglass strips are for, and the answer loops between teams. That is exactly the kind of circular requirement the skill should surface.

Why this matters for the skill:

- it is more vivid than generic "don't automate too early"
- it demonstrates cross-team requirement drift

### Case 3: Nevada and Fremont automation mistake

The mirror gives a direct retrospective: the big mistake in Nevada and Fremont was automating too early.

Why this matters for the skill:

- it strengthens the skill's guardrail against premature AI or workflow automation
- it is a highly reusable lesson for modern users who want to automate messy business processes

### Case 4: machine that makes the machine

Tesla's official master plan provides first-party context for factory iteration and manufacturing speed:

- factory design is itself a product
- the goal is a 5x to 10x improvement on a multi-iteration cycle

Why this matters for the skill:

- it explains why cycle time is not just "go faster"
- it gives a first-party bridge from strategy to manufacturing execution

## Weak or overstated claims to avoid

- Do not claim the five-step algorithm already existed during PayPal in its mature form.
- Do not present every popular Musk anecdote as directly sourced from primary materials.
- Do not overquote the book mirror as if it were an official publisher transcript.

## Recommended skill changes

- strengthen the named-person requirement rule in step 1
- add at least one explicit anti-pattern example for "automating circular requirements"
- use the fiberglass-strip anecdote as an illustrative case
- keep the output operational and anti-fluff
